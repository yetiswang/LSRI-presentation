The Challenge

AI and lab automation can transform materials research. But today's efforts are fragmented — parallel, disconnected systems that fall short of the integration needed for real scientific impact.

DiscoveryLabNL is TU/e's answer: a shared infrastructure for autonomous materials discovery, integrating self-driving laboratories, advanced multimodal characterisation, and an AI Core facility to accelerate the design, synthesis, and validation of next-generation materials.

Built on IPM (NWO Gravitation) and Big Chemistry (NGF) foundations at ICMS

Three Pillars, One Closed Loop

Self-Driving Laboratories

Robotic synthesis, automated sample handling, in-line sensing. AI-guided closed-loop experimentation across a hub-and-spoke SDL network.

Advanced Characterisation

Optical, electron, and X-ray microscopy combined with spectroscopy. The continuous verification and integration layer of the discovery loop.

AI Core Facility

FAIR data backbone, curated model catalogue, dedicated AI science team. Federated design serving the full consortium.

The Autonomous Discovery Loop

Discovery Domains

Priority research domains where autonomous closed-loop experimentation can deliver transformative impact.

Soft Matter & Biomaterials

Responsive hydrogels, self-assembling systems, and biocompatible materials for diagnostics, implants, and drug delivery.

Sustainable Energy

Accelerated design of electrolytes, electrode architectures, photocatalysts, and solar fuel systems for the energy transition.

Circular Materials

AI-guided discovery of recyclable, degradable, and bio-based polymers, composites, and inorganic systems.

Molecular & Supramolecular Systems

High-throughput exploration of molecular libraries for coatings, optoelectronics, and precision functional materials.

Synthetic Biology Exploratory

Programmable living systems for on-demand biosynthesis of functional materials and therapeutic molecules.

The Global Race

64+ initiatives · 17 countries · $10B+ combined investment

Every major science nation has moved. Autonomous chemical synthesis is the fastest-moving frontier — Jensen (MIT), Cronin/Chemify (Glasgow), Burke/MMLI (Illinois), Hein/Telescope (UBC). Continental Europe has one synthesis SDL group: Timothy Noël at UvA. The Netherlands has Big Chemistry — but no full-stack materials discovery infrastructure.

Smart microscopy, autonomous instruments, AI-driven spectroscopy — groups worldwide advance single techniques in isolation. No one integrates them into a multimodal closed loop. That requires cross-domain coordination no single group can achieve alone.

Positioning in the LSRI Landscape

Engineering

  • Every commercial SDL that went industrial needed process engineering for scale-up
  • Flow, heat transfer, mixing, mechanical testing — the missing layer academic SDLs lack
  • Overlaps with the Engineering LSRI roadmap — automation, robotics, and integrated instrumentation

Materials

  • Overlaps with the Materials LSRI roadmap — accelerated design and characterisation of advanced materials
  • DIFFER as federated partner: molecular screening tools and autonomous electron microscopy feed into the shared discovery loop

Life Science & Enabling Technologies

  • Overlaps with the LS&ET LSRI roadmap — bio-inspired and biocompatible materials discovery
  • Shared instrumentation for life science characterisation and enabling technology platforms

TU/e’s campus consortium spans three LSRI roadmaps. One shared infrastructure, many domains.
